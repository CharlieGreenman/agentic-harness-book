# Project Guidelines

## Communication Preferences
- When showing text to the user for review, use plain text only. Avoid italics, bold, and blockquotes — they render in colors that are hard to read against the user's terminal background.

## Author Context
- Author was born in 1991. Do not mention this in the book, but use it as a reference for which anecdotes and perspectives are relatable. Avoid first-person anecdotes about life before the internet or other experiences the author would not have had.

## Book Writing Tone
- First-person, conversational, honest. The author thinking out loud.
- Direct and plainspoken. No academic jargon or corporate-speak.
- Comfortable admitting uncertainty and confusion.
- Uses concrete analogies to ground abstract ideas.
- Short punchy sentences mixed with longer ones. Not afraid of one-line paragraphs for emphasis.
- Speaks to the reader as an equal exploring alongside the author, not being lectured to.
- Give humans the benefit of the doubt and credit. Avoid framing people as oblivious or behind. People are generally aware and trying to make sense of things with the information they have.
- Keep examples and references digestible for the average person. Where technical precision matters (this book is about agent harnesses), explain the jargon the first time it appears.
- Do not begin sentences with "And".
- Do not begin sentences with "But". Use "However," instead when needed.
- Do not begin sentences with "Because". Use "The" or rephrase instead.

## Book Writing Process
- When writing a new chapter, always go through it paragraph by paragraph with the user for review before considering it final.

## Git
- Do not add "Co-Authored-By: Claude Opus 4.6" to commit messages. Only the user should be attributed.
